Lessons learned: 
- het is zeer makkelijk om jezelf te overladen met werk en zeer moeilijk om nee te zeggen; maar als je geen buffers vrijhoudt tussen je opdrachten (als een opdracht uitloopt, als je tijd wil om nieuwe zaken te leren, tijd om te rusten) kom je in de problemen terecht. Zelfs ls je buffers vrijhoudt kan je in de problemen terechtkomen: een klant die enthousiast is over je werk en verder wil gaan op dat lan kan je niet zomaar afwijzen, hoewel je het eigenlijk zou moeten doen.
- het meeste voldoening haal je uit werk dat (a) sociaal belangrijk is (cf. DevelopmentSeed, TOPP maar evengoed RSS), (b) dat je hl goed kan doen © dat voor jezelf is. Dat betekent dat je als freelancer eigenlijk beter werk aanneemt dat je capaciteiten niet tot het uiterste strekt, zodat je de tijd en de expertise hebt om echt van een website iets bijzonders te maken en je niet moet zweten om tot een resultaat te komen dat uiteindelijk toch maar middelmatig is. Dat betekent niet dat je het jezelf gemakkelijk moet maken, maar dat je de uitdaging moet zoeken in een perfecte afwerking en een paar verrassende twists, het 'opbouwen' van de site zelf moet een makkie zijn.

Je moet immers niet alleen de site kunnen maken, maar ook de nodige expertise kunnen aanreiken over mogelijke problemen, best practices, suggesties in verband met het opzet van het project, je moet tijdens het maken van de website meerdere alternatieven kunnen aanbieden, anders kunnen clinten soms eindeloos zitten piekeren over iets dat jij met een paar uur programmeren meteen kan presenteren zodat men alleen nog maar te _kijken_ heeft of iets 'werkt' of niet werkt. Dus, hoe graag je jezelf ook steeds wat verder pusht, het is niet eerlijk tegenover je clint als je dat niet kan bieden.

Dat betekent ook dat je beter mensen zoekt om mee samen te werken, want niemand is tegelijk goed in de zakelijke kant, het programmeren, het ontwerpen en het plannen.

Als beginnende freelancer is het ook moeilijk om aan te voelen hoe je met je klanten moet omgaan. Enerzijds heb jij de expertise en wil je dus dat zij ook naar je expertise luisteren en hun plannen aanpassen op basis van wat jij weet uit ervaring dat wel werkt en wat niet werkt.

Samenwerken met andere freelancers is ook niet zo gemakkelijk als het lijkt. Je moet mensen vinden die je goed complementeren, als je die gevonden hebt moeten ze de nodige tijd vrijhebben, en je moet echt met elkaar kunnen samenwerken, wat een communicatie-overhead impliceert. De neiging om zaken uit te besteden en er dan niet naar te kijken en te hopen dat het resultaat iet of wat deftig is, leidt enkel tot miserie.

I've long been suspicious of nonprofit journalism, and even more suspicious of government-sponsored journalism. Like Kevin Davis of the Investigative News Network said a while back, "Being nonprofit is not a business model, it's a tax status." Proponents defend non-profits by saying it allows one to do a better, purer kind of journalism that a commercial enterprise simply would not support. In reality, the lure of the nonprofit is that it promises an environment where you can just focus on the journalism without having to worry about money, or in fact about having anyone at all to read whatever you write. It seems lazy to me.

But just as we've moved from talking about why paywalls can never work to discussing the specific circumstances in which they can thrive, and from talking about whether social media has a place in journalism to talking about how to integrate and how not to integrate social media in our reporting, I feel it's time to kick it up a notch in our discussion of non-profit journalism as well, and talk about when it works, when it's stupid and when it's really needed. When does it better communities, not just grizzled journalists' bank accounts?

I believe there's a place for noncommercial professional journalism in society, in three varieties: philantropic, subsidized and civil.


When it comes to state-sponsored media, I think there's a place for it but I also think it should operate like ProPublica does: put creative commons on everything. The purpose of being a non-profit is to provide a good to society, and you can only do that if your information is spread as widely as possible.






From the perspective of creating a healthy information society, I wonder if it would make sense for the government to invest in social media training for these local business owners, as well as highlighting some of these messages by acting as an aggregator and promotor itself. And finding ways for individuals to spread their message too, without having to commit to publishing a blog of even a full-blown blogpost. It's not that newspapers and news websites can't do that sort of stuff, but they're too often a useless middle man, there's better ways to spend their time. Same thing for community calendars. And in communities where city, county or state is not forthcoming, business owners can band together. Some already do, e.g. for shopping streets / districts, but it needs to be (1) broader than that because people won't subscribe to a zillion different Facebook pages for things they're only mildly interested in and (2) it needs to make more use of communication channels that already exist, meaning: stop creating websites nobody knows about, use Facebook instead. And not just local businesses. You could have city aggregators for adult education (evening school opportunities, workshops etc.), music and such. And they could publish on a website too because more platforms is always a good thing, but it's not clear why you'd need all that stuff on a newspaper website, what the advantage is to all that. News organizations could play a big role in this, but not by being the aggregator (certainly not in the haphazard way that local newspapers "curate" this kind of news right now), instead by providing the media training, and in a world where everyone is a publisher, we need a *lot* of media training. Noncommercial and commercial professional journalism should support noncommercial layman journalism.

* Stories sometimes point to certain sources of information for everything from municipal affairs to evening classes. But if you didn't see the story (or if you forget about it) you won't know about it. A better way to handle this sort of thing is with a portal. Ideally, this is something governments provide, and actually a lot of them do a reasonable job (except they sometimes limit their information to stuff that's strictly gov-related) but the main problem is that those information portals are often not as widely known as they should be, and you don't make the unfamiliar known by promoting it on yet another unfamiliar website. (This is actually a big problem in Belgium, which has a bigger government than the US, and e.g. finding what sort of subsidies you can get for what sort of things is truly hard.)





* Obituaries and milestones are another: these have traditionally been part of the newspaper (like public notices, classifieds, coupons, advertising) because the newspaper was the only high-frequency high-circulation medium we had with a high-enough information density (contrast with TV) to allow for it. With the internet, all that has changed. People still pay their local newspaper to put in an obit or milestone, but there's no reason why a small community-run website couldn't do that (and for a more urban crowd, why Facebook couldn't do that). Doesn't mean a news organization shouldn't, but there's no intrinsic reason why it's part of the package, and indeed news organizations' need to profit can make these services more expensive than they need to be and thus ripe for disruption.

* Why working for companies sucks *

If everything that's even slightly important (like a paper's redesign, a company reorg, product strategy, whatever) gets outsourced to a consultancy because a company doesn't trust its own employees, then consultancies, freelance shops and design shops are where people will want to work, and the quality of your in-house staff goes down.
